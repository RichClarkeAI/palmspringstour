You've arrived at the Grace Lewis Miller Residence at 2311 North Indian Canyon Drive — Richard Neutra's very first commission in Palm Springs, completed in 1937. This unassuming little house is, in many ways, where Palm Springs modernism began.

To appreciate this house, you need to know about its remarkable client. Grace Darling Lewis Miller was born in 1895, a cultured woman from St. Louis who married physician Herman Benjamin Miller in 1918. When her husband passed away in 1935, Grace's life changed dramatically. At forty years old, recently widowed with two sons, she reinvented herself completely.

Grace became a devoted practitioner of something called the Mensendieck System — a European method of functional body exercises developed by Dr. Bess Mensendieck, an American physician who had earned her medical degree in Zurich and opened back therapy studios across Europe. The system focused on proper posture, spinal health, and body awareness through slow, deliberate movements practiced before mirrors. Grace brought this discipline to Palm Springs and taught it to wealthy clients at a time when the desert town was still rough and unfinished.

She was also no intellectual lightweight. After her husband's death, she earned a Master's degree in American history from Washington University and spent nearly thirty years researching the life of explorer Meriwether Lewis, producing scholarly articles and even a documentary film script.

So when Grace drove to Palm Springs in July 1936 and purchased a two-and-a-half-acre lot on what was then called North Indian Avenue, she knew exactly what she wanted. Within a month, she had commissioned Richard Neutra to design a combined home and studio. She told him plainly: "I did not want a Rubens. I wanted a Picasso."

The story goes that Neutra arrived at the site towing a trailer carrying a rotating drafting table and an awning — a clever device that let him study the rotational angles of sun and wind throughout the day. While he sat in the shade drawing, his wife Dione played the cello in the sun. It's a beautiful image — the architect and the musician, reading the desert together.

The house was designed and built in just six months. At only 108 square meters — about 1,160 square feet — and costing just $7,500, it's one of the smallest homes on this tour. But don't let its size fool you. Neutra himself considered it one of his greatest achievements, precisely because the result revealed what happens when a brilliant architect collaborates with a splendid client.

Grace's profession shaped every inch of the design. The house functioned as both living space and teaching studio for her Mensendieck classes. One entire wall of the living room was mirrored so her students could observe their own posture and movements. The open floor plan drew on what Neutra called the Japanese concept of zashiki — flexible space that transforms depending on the circumstances. Rooms weren't labeled "living room" or "dining room" in any traditional sense. As Neutra optimistically put it: "Our rooms are large portions of space in which we live, and are pragmatically elastic."

In fact, the house had no dining room at all. Grace liked to eat outside — a radical idea in 1937 that would become a defining feature of desert living.

Look at how Neutra handled the desert environment. The northeast corner features white stucco walls that step backward, echoing the traditional adobe architecture of Pueblo Indians — a deliberate nod to indigenous desert building wisdom. But the southeast corner opens up completely in clear glass, with sliding panels that reveal a reflecting pool right outside. Grace was enchanted by this feature. "The water attenuates the effect of the brightness of the sun," she wrote in 1938, "and sometimes causes the nicest reflections dancing on the roof of the hall and the porch."

That reflecting pool wasn't just decorative — it was therapeutic. The dancing light on the white ceiling created a naturally calming atmosphere for her exercise students. It's an early example of what we'd now call biophilic design — using natural elements to promote wellbeing.

Every detail responded to Grace's specific needs. Neutra designed her wardrobe with seven separate compartments, each at slightly different depths depending on the clothing category. He asked her: "How many sweaters do you have? How many hats?" The result was cabinetry tailored like a bespoke suit.

For her morning routine, he created a tiny bathroom illuminated by northern light through translucent glass — soft, even light that flattered the skin. Her makeup table was a sheet of onyx with ivory streaks, carefully positioned to catch that gentle illumination.

Even the bedroom was designed for her sensory pleasure. It contained a pair of French doors that could be bolted open at right angles to form a tiny private porch. When she went to sleep, she could enjoy the fragrance of citrus trees she had planted just outside — Neutra understood that architecture engages all the senses, not just sight.

For the exercise studio, Neutra designed glass walls stretching nearly from floor to ceiling, flooding the space with natural light. But he also studied the wind patterns carefully, narrowing the openings on the north side to block the strong winds that carried sand across the desert floor.

The house was one of the earliest Neutra projects to be photographed by Julius Shulman, whose images would later make Palm Springs modernism famous worldwide. Those early photographs show a small white box floating in the vast, empty desert — windswept brush stretching to the mountains in every direction. Today, of course, the surrounding neighborhood has filled in, but you can still appreciate how revolutionary this little building must have looked in 1937.

For many years after Grace returned to St. Louis permanently around 1960, the house suffered as a rental property. Insensitive changes accumulated. But fortunately, a later owner undertook a careful restoration, returning the house to something close to Neutra's original vision.

In 2002, the Grace Lewis Miller House was designated a Palm Springs Historic Site — a recognition of its importance not just as architecture, but as the starting point of an entire movement. Without this commission, Neutra might never have returned to Palm Springs. And without Neutra's Palm Springs work, there might be no Kaufmann House, no desert modernism as we know it.

As you view the house from the street, notice how it sits low and quiet on its lot. It doesn't announce itself. There's no dramatic roofline, no steel frame spanning impossible distances. Instead, it whispers. It's architecture as a gentle collaboration between a disciplined woman, a visionary architect, and the desert itself. Sometimes the most influential buildings aren't the loudest ones — they're the ones that get the details right.

The parking tip for this stop: look for street parking at the corner of North Indian Canyon Drive and Rochelle Road, then walk about two doors south. You'll see the facade clearly from the sidewalk. As always, please be respectful — this is a private residence.

Before you move on, take a moment to imagine Grace Miller here in the late 1930s, teaching her students to stand tall and move with intention in a house that was itself designed to help people live better. She and Neutra shared the same belief: that our surroundings shape who we are. This little house is proof that even a small building, thoughtfully designed, can change the world.
