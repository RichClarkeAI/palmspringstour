You're now approaching the Kaufmann Residence - arguably the most famous mid-century modern home in Palm Springs, and certainly one of the most photographed houses in the world.

This masterpiece was designed by Austrian-American architect Richard Neutra in 1946 for Edgar J. Kaufmann Sr., the wealthy owner of Kaufmann's Department Store in Pittsburgh. Now, Edgar Kaufmann was no ordinary client. A decade earlier, in 1935, he had commissioned Frank Lloyd Wright to design Fallingwater, that stunning house cantilevered over a waterfall in Pennsylvania - one of the most celebrated houses of the 20th century.

So when Kaufmann decided he wanted a winter retreat in Palm Springs, architecture enthusiasts everywhere held their breath. Would he choose Wright again? Wright certainly expected the commission. He had strong opinions about desert architecture and had already designed several notable buildings.

But Kaufmann chose Richard Neutra instead.

This decision created quite a controversy in architectural circles. Wright was not pleased. Neutra and Wright had a complicated relationship - they had worked together briefly in the 1920s, but their philosophies diverged significantly. Neutra's approach was more scientifically oriented, focused on how architecture affects human psychology and physiology, while Wright was more romantic and intuitive.

Richard Neutra was born in Vienna in 1892 and studied under Adolf Loos, one of modernism's founding figures. He moved to the United States in 1923, worked briefly with Wright and Rudolf Schindler, and established his own practice in Los Angeles by 1930. Neutra became known for what he called "biorealism" - design that supports human well-being by integrating architecture with the natural environment.

The Kaufmann House was completed in 1947, and it's a stunning example of Neutra's philosophy. The house sprawls across the desert in a pinwheel-shaped plan, with wings extending outward to capture different views and create outdoor living spaces. Neutra himself somewhat critically called it "grandiose waste" - but he meant that as a comment on its generous proportions, not a criticism of its design.

The house is built from steel, glass, and native stone quarried from the nearby mountains. The stone walls ground the house in the landscape, while walls of glass dissolve the boundary between inside and outside. The steel frame allows for expansive overhangs that provide crucial shade in the harsh desert sun.

One of Neutra's key innovations was understanding that in the desert, you need to control both sun and views independently. The house uses louvers and overhangs precisely calculated to block the high summer sun while allowing the lower winter sun to warm the interiors. At the same time, the glass walls frame views of the San Jacinto Mountains and the surrounding desert.

The heart of the house is the central living area, which opens onto a spectacular pool and patio. This indoor-outdoor flow was revolutionary for its time and became a defining feature of Palm Springs architecture. The pool house serves as a viewing pavilion, creating a dialogue between two structures across the water.

Now, here's where the house enters popular culture. In 1960, photographer Slim Aarons captured what would become one of the most iconic images of mid-century American life: "Poolside Gossip." The photograph shows three elegantly dressed women seated by the pool, with the Kaufmann House gleaming behind them. This image, more than any architectural writing, captured the imagination of the public and came to symbolize the glamour and sophistication of Palm Springs life.

But the house has had an eventful history since Kaufmann died in 1955. It stood vacant for several years, then passed through a series of owners including singer Barry Manilow and San Diego Chargers owner Eugene Klein. Over the years, insensitive renovations accumulated: a patio was enclosed, floral wallpaper was added to bedrooms, a wall was removed for a media room, and air conditioning units altered the roof lines.

In 1992, the house faced its greatest crisis. It was listed for sale for three and a half years before being purchased by Brent Harris, an investment manager, and his wife Beth Edwards Harris, an architectural historian, for just $1.5 million. That might sound expensive, but for a Neutra masterpiece, it was a bargain - a sign of how poorly the house had been maintained.

What followed was one of the most remarkable restoration projects in American architecture. The Harrises spent five years returning the house to Neutra's original vision. They researched extensively through the Neutra archives at UCLA and Columbia University. They even worked with Julius Shulman, the architectural photographer who had documented the house in 1947, accessing his unpublished interior photos.

The restoration was meticulous. The Harrises obtained original paint formulas and fixtures from suppliers. They purchased a metal-crimping machine to reproduce the sheet-metal fascia that originally lined the roof. Perhaps most remarkably, they had a long-closed section of a Utah quarry reopened to mine matching stone to replace what had been damaged.

They didn't just restore the house - they restored its setting. The Harrises purchased several adjoining plots to more than double the land around the 3,200-square-foot house, recreating the desert buffer that Neutra had envisioned. They rebuilt the pool house and maintained the tennis court on an added parcel.

In 2008, after the Harrises divorced, the house was auctioned by Christie's as part of a high-profile contemporary art sale. It sold for $15 million, though the sale later fell through when the bidder breached the purchase agreement. The house was listed again in October 2020 for $25 million - a testament to how values of mid-century modern masterpieces have appreciated.

Despite being in Palm Springs, the Kaufmann House was included in a Los Angeles Times survey as one of the top 10 houses of all time in Los Angeles. More recently, it served as a filming location for the 2022 film "Don't Worry Darling," introducing it to a new generation.

As you view the house from the street - and please be respectful, as this is a private residence - notice how Neutra positioned it on the site. The house seems to float above the desert floor, connected yet separate from its surroundings. The horizontal lines echo the desert floor, while the vertical elements frame views of the mountains.

Look for the precision of the steelwork, the way the stone walls seem to grow from the ground, and the subtle variations in the roof lines that create different qualities of light and shadow throughout the day. This is architecture as both art and science - beautiful to look at, but also a sophisticated machine for living in a challenging environment.

The Kaufmann House represents a pivotal moment in American architecture. It showed that modernism could be luxurious and glamorous, not austere and cold. It demonstrated how to build sensitively in fragile desert ecosystems. And it proved that with enough vision, even the most challenging environments could become places of beauty and comfort.

Edgar Kaufmann's choice of Neutra over Wright proved prescient. While Wright's dramatic Fallingwater is a masterpiece, Neutra's Kaufmann House has had perhaps greater influence on how we live today. Its blend of indoor and outdoor spaces, its environmental sensitivity, its embrace of technology and nature - these ideas continue to shape residential architecture around the world.

Stand here for a moment and imagine the Hollywood parties, the celebrity guests, the elegant life captured in Slim Aarons' photograph. This isn't just a house - it's a time capsule of mid-century American optimism, when anything seemed possible and the future looked bright and beautiful.

This is the house that put Palm Springs on the architectural map. From here, every other house you'll visit on this tour owes something to the path that Richard Neutra and Edgar Kaufmann blazed together in the desert.
